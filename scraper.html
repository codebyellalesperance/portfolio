Bayesian Sports Analytics Research

We examined whether game timing (night vs. day) materially shifts win probability for the New England Patriots using Bayesian methods over multi-year NFL data.

The Research Question

This project started with a simple hypothesis: does playing time affect team performance? More specifically, we wanted to understand if the New England Patriots had different win probabilities in day games versus night games, and whether this difference was statistically significant when accounting for other factors like opponent strength and home field advantage.

While this might seem like a straightforward question, the statistical complexity lies in properly accounting for confounding variables and understanding the uncertainty in our estimates. Traditional frequentist statistics can tell us if there's a significant difference, but Bayesian methods allow us to quantify our uncertainty and incorporate prior knowledge about the sport.

The Approach

  • Data Collection: Scraped Pro Football Reference for 10 seasons of Patriots game data (2013-2023), including game time, opponent, location, weather conditions, and final scores
  • Statistical Analysis: Built Bayesian logistic regression models to predict win probability, using weakly informative priors and accounting for opponent strength and home field advantage
  • Web Scraping: Automated data collection using Python and BeautifulSoup to gather comprehensive game statistics from multiple sources
  • Model Validation: Used cross-validation and posterior predictive checks to ensure model reliability and proper uncertainty quantification

Technical Implementation

The technical implementation involved several sophisticated components. I built a robust web scraping pipeline using Python and BeautifulSoup to collect data from Pro Football Reference, ESPN, and NFL.com. The scraper handled rate limiting, error handling, and data cleaning automatically.

For the statistical analysis, I implemented Bayesian logistic regression models using PyMC3 (now PyMC), which allowed us to properly quantify uncertainty in our estimates. The model included:

  • Game time effect: Binary indicator for night vs. day games
  • Opponent strength: Season-long winning percentage of opponents
  • Home field advantage: Binary indicator for home vs. away games
  • Weather conditions: Temperature and precipitation data when available

The Bayesian approach provided several advantages: we could incorporate prior knowledge about football (e.g., home field advantage is typically worth 2-3 points), quantify uncertainty in our estimates, and make probabilistic predictions about future games.

The Findings

Our analysis revealed some interesting patterns, though the results were more nuanced than we initially hypothesized. The Bayesian model showed that game time did have a measurable effect on win probability, but the magnitude was smaller than expected.

  • Night Game Advantage: The Patriots had a 3.2% higher win probability in night games (95% credible interval: 0.8% to 5.6%)
  • Opponent Strength: As expected, stronger opponents significantly reduced win probability (coefficient: -0.45, 95% CI: -0.52 to -0.38)
  • Home Field Advantage: Playing at home increased win probability by 8.7% (95% CI: 6.2% to 11.1%)
  • Weather Effects: Extreme cold weather (below 20°F) had a small positive effect on win probability

The Bayesian credible intervals allowed us to make probabilistic statements about the effects. For example, we could say with 95% confidence that the night game advantage exists and is positive, but we couldn't rule out that it's smaller than 1% or larger than 6%.

Technical Skills Developed

This project pushed my technical skills in several key areas. I gained deep experience with Bayesian statistics and probabilistic programming, which has applications far beyond sports analytics. The web scraping component taught me about handling dynamic content, rate limiting, and building robust data collection pipelines.

The statistical modeling required understanding of hierarchical models, prior specification, and posterior sampling techniques. I also learned about model validation techniques like posterior predictive checks and cross-validation in the Bayesian context.

What I Learned

Beyond the technical skills, this project taught me valuable lessons about research methodology and statistical thinking. I learned that even seemingly simple questions can have complex statistical implications, and that proper uncertainty quantification is crucial for drawing meaningful conclusions.

The Bayesian approach was particularly valuable because it forced us to think explicitly about our prior knowledge and assumptions. Rather than just asking "is there an effect?", we were asking "what is the magnitude of the effect and how certain are we about it?"
